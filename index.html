    Understanding Cognitive Distortions:

    • All-or-Nothing Thinking: Seeing things in black and white, with no middle ground. You view situations as all good or all bad, without considering the shades of gray.
    • Catastrophizing: Exaggerating the negative aspects of a situation and expecting the worst possible outcome, even when it's unlikely to happen.
    • Mind-Reading: Assuming you know what others are thinking or feeling without sufficient evidence, often leading to misinterpretations.
    • Overgeneralization: Drawing broad negative conclusions based on one or a few isolated incidents.
    • Labeling: Using negative labels to describe oneself or others based on mistakes or behavior.
    • Personalization: Taking responsibility for events or situations that are beyond one's control.
    • Discounting the Positive: Minimizing or ignoring positive experiences or qualities and focusing only on the negative.
    • Emotional Reasoning: Believing that feelings reflect objective reality.
    • Should Statements: Using "should," "must," or "ought to" statements that create unrealistic expectations for oneself or others.
    • Fortune Telling: Predicting negative outcomes with certainty, even when there's no evidence to support it.
    • Magnification and Minimization: Exaggerating the importance of negative events (magnification) or minimizing the importance of positive events (minimization).
    • Control Fallacies: Believing that one is either completely powerless or entirely responsible for everything that happens.
